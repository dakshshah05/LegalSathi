The Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005 (Women's Property Rights)
This historic amendment in Indian personal law established equal property inheritance rights for daughters and mothers, resolving historical gender biases in joint family systems.

Key Legal Rights under the Property Act:
1. Coparcenary by Birth: A daughter has the same coparcenary rights (joint ownership by birth) in the ancestral property of a Hindu Undivided Family (HUF) as a son. She has the same rights, liabilities, and shares as if she had been a son.
2. Marital Status: A daughter's property rights are absolute and permanent. Whether she is unmarried, married, widowed, or divorced, she continues to hold her share in her parental ancestral property.
3. Status of Father: The Supreme Court in the landmark judgment Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020) clarified that the right of a coparcener is by birth. Thus, the daughter holds this right even if her father died before the amendment took effect on September 9, 2005.
4. Ancestral vs. Self-Acquired Property:
   - Ancestral Property: Inherited up to four generations of lineage without partition. A daughter has an birthright and cannot be excluded via a father's will.
   - Self-Acquired Property: Bought by an individual using their own money. The owner can will or gift it to anyone they choose. However, if the father dies without a will (intestate), the daughter, mother, and wife inherit equal shares alongside the son as Class I heirs.
5. Stridhan: A woman holds absolute, exclusive ownership over her Stridhan (gifts, jewellery, cash, and assets given to her at the time of marriage by parents, in-laws, or relatives). No family member or husband has any claim over it.
6. Legal Remedies: If denied her inheritance, a daughter can file a partition suit in a civil court to secure her division and obtain a stay order on the sale of ancestral assets.
