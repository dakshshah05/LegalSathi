The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (DV Act)
This civil legislation provides comprehensive legal safeguards and remedies for women experiencing abuse, harassment, or violence in a domestic or matrimonial relationship.

Core Protective Provisions under the DV Act:
1. Broad Definition of Abuse: The law defines domestic violence beyond physical harm. It covers:
   - Physical Abuse: Bodily pain, assault, force, or threats.
   - Sexual Abuse: Forced sexual activity or harassment.
   - Verbal & Emotional Abuse: Insults, name-calling, threats to the woman or her loved ones, and constant humiliation.
   - Economic Abuse: Depriving the woman of financial support, restricting access to shared assets, taking away her dowry (Stridhan) or jewellery, and locking her out of the home.
2. Right to Reside: Under Section 17, a woman holds the absolute right to reside in the shared household (matrimonial or ancestral home). She cannot be evicted or thrown out by her husband or in-laws without a proper magistrate order.
3. Protection Orders: Magistrates can issue legal orders stopping the respondent (husband/relatives) from repeating acts of violence, visiting the woman's office, or communicating with her. Breach of a Protection Order is a criminal, non-bailable offense.
4. Residence Orders: The court can direct that the woman be reinstated in the home, order the abuser to vacate the residence, or force the abuser to pay for alternative accommodation.
5. Monetary & Custody Relief: A woman can claim compensation for medical expenses, loss of income, general maintenance, and temporary custody of her children.
6. Support Networks: Victims can contact Protection Officers, One Stop Centres (OSCs), or registered shelter houses to seek emergency aid and file a Domestic Incident Report (DIR).
