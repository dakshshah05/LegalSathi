Divorce and Maintenance Rights in India
These legal guidelines outline the procedure for marriage dissolution, alimony, custody, and securing maintenance under secular and personal laws in India.

Key Divorce Rules:
1. Mutual Consent Divorce: Under Section 13B of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, if both spouses mutually agree that the marriage cannot survive, they can file a joint petition. This is the fastest, most dignified route. There is a standard 6-month cooling period, which can be waived by a judge under specific criteria.
2. Contested Divorce: If one spouse opposes, divorce can be filed based on grounds including cruelty (bodily or mental harm), desertion (leaving without excuse for 2+ years), adultery, conversion, or unsoundness of mind.
3. Maintenance (Section 125 CrPC / Section 144 BNSS): This is a secular provision applicable to all Indian citizens regardless of religion. A wife who is unable to support herself financially can claim monthly maintenance from a husband who has sufficient resources.
4. Working Wives: A woman can still claim maintenance even if she is earning a salary, provided her income is insufficient to maintain the standard of life she had in the matrimonial household.
5. Alimony: Temporary maintenance (interim) is paid during court trials to meet legal fees and basic needs. Permanent alimony is settled at the end of the divorce, calculated normally at 20% to 25% of the husband's net salary or as a lump-sum amount.
6. Child Custody: Decided purely on the basis of the child's welfare, health, and education. Typically, physical custody of children under 5 is awarded to the mother, though fathers are granted visiting rights.
7. Right to Residence: A wife has the legal right to reside in her matrimonial home during the divorce litigation, even if the house is owned or rented solely by the husband.
